SUMMARY

Analysis of legal breaches in compliance and public disclosure

I. MANDATORY PUBLIC DISCLOSURE

  • Under 26 U.S. Code § 6104(d), 501(c)(3) nonprofits must provide:
  • a) Copies of their three most recent IRS Form 990s (or 990-PF if private foundation).
  • b) IRS determination letter.
  • These documents must be provided to any requester, without the need to identify themselves or explain the purpose.
  • REFUSAL TO COMPLY IS A VIOLATION OF FEDERAL LAW.
  • Penalties: 26 U.S. Code §7210 (failure to provide access results in $20/day fines, capped at $10,000).

REFERENCES:

  • https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/6104
  • https://www.irs.gov/charities-non-profits/public-disclosure-and-availability-of-exempt-organizations-returns-and-applications

II. FILING REQUIREMENTS

  • IRS Form 990 (or 990-PF) must be filed annually.
  • Failure to file for three consecutive years results in automatic revocation of tax-exempt status (per IRS Rev. Proc. 2011-15).
  • New Mexico requires filing with the Charities Division and Attorney General.

REFERENCES:

  • https://www.irs.gov/charities-non-profits/annual-reporting-and-filing-requirements
  • https://www.nmag.gov/charities-division.aspx
III. Global Operations and Solicitation Laws

A U.S. 501(c)(3) can operate internationally, but must:

  • Maintain control over how funds are used overseas.
  • Report foreign activities and grants via Schedule F on the 990/990-PF.
  • Follow U.S. anti-terrorism laws (e.g., OFAC compliance).

Soliciting donations from international donors does not exempt the organization from U.S. laws.

Sources:

  • IRS Form 990, Schedule F
  • IRS 990 Filing Requirements
IV. Misrepresentation and Harassment

Making false legal claims to dissuade legitimate inquiries is considered deceptive and may violate:

  • 15 U.S. Code § 45 (FTC Act) - Unfair or deceptive acts/practices.
  • State consumer protection laws.
THREATS:

Threatening violence (e.g., "would be shot if asked for my tax returns on the street") can violate criminal law. If made across state/national lines, may fall under:

  • 18 U.S. Code § 875 - Interstate communications.
  • 47 U.S. Code § 223 - Harassing communications.

Also potentially actionable as civil harassment, intimidation, or incitement.

Sources:

  • FTC Act - 15 U.S. Code § 45
  • 18 U.S. Code § 875 - Interstate Communications
  • 47 U.S. Code § 223 - Harassing Communications
V. Anonymity of Requesters

Federal law does not allow charities to require identification to receive Form 990 documents. The IRS explicitly allows anonymous requests in-person or via mail/email.

Refusing to provide documents based on anonymity is illegal.

Source:

  • 26 U.S. Code § 6104 - Public Disclosure of Exempt Organizations' Forms
VI. Retaliation Against Whistleblowers

Harassment, defamation, or retaliation against individuals asking questions may violate:

  • Federal whistleblower protections (e.g., Sarbanes-Oxley Act for nonprofits under certain grants).
  • State civil rights or defamation laws.
VII. Fraudulent Representation

False claims about nonprofit governance, legal rights, or compliance may be considered:

  • Mail or wire fraud (18 U.S. Code § 1341).
  • Misrepresentation to donors (state-level charity fraud statutes).
  • Unauthorized legal advice or impersonation of attorneys.

Source:

  • 18 U.S. Code § 1341 - Mail Fraud
VIII. Failure to Track Financial Expenditures

Admitting to making "hardship payments" without any recordkeeping is a serious breach of fiduciary duty.

Under 26 CFR § 1.501(c)(3)-1(c), a charity must ensure funds are used exclusively for exempt purposes. Lack of documentation for expenditures can result in revocation of tax-exempt status and potential liability for misuse of charitable assets.

May also trigger an IRS audit under Form 990 Schedule F (foreign activities and distributions).

Source:

  • 26 CFR § 1.501(c)(3)-1(c) - Expenditures and Fiduciary Duty

IX. CONCLUSION

  • This organization appears to be in breach of:
  • - Federal tax law (disclosure, annual filing, financial tracking).
  • - State nonprofit registration law (New Mexico).
  • - FTC deceptive practice law.
  • - Possibly criminal law (interstate threats, retaliation, obstruction).
