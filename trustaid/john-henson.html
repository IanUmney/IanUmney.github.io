Intimidating and Retalitory Threats Against Critics

Chilling Free Speech & Public Accountability
  • Charities exist for public benefit and are expected to be transparent and open to scrutiny.
  • Creating a list of critics, especially if linked to threats of legal action, can intimidate whistleblowers, donors, or journalists.
  • This undermines public trust and could deter people from asking legitimate questions or reporting wrongdoing.
Legal Risk for Retaliation

If the charity retaliates against whistleblowers or critics (e.g., former staff, donors, or journalists), it may violate:

  • Whistleblower protection laws
  • Defamation or anti-SLAPP laws (which protect individuals from strategic lawsuits intended to silence them)
  • Nonprofit governance rules about ethical conduct and fiduciary duty

False and Deplorable Insults and Rumours

Defamation and Libel

To prove defamation (specifically libel if written, or slander if spoken), the statement must be:

  • False – The accusation is not true.
  • Published or communicated – It was shared with someone other than the person it’s about.
  • Harmful – It damages the person’s reputation, career, or safety.
  • Made negligently or maliciously – The speaker knew or should have known it was false or acted recklessly.

Accusations of pedophilia or child trafficking are considered defamation per se, meaning harm is presumed — the victim doesn’t need to prove specific damage (e.g., lost income or reputation), because the claim is so severe and inherently damaging.
